Avocado is the one of the hottest health food out there right now; raw almonds, quinoa, hummus are main actors on social media eating style. Put that green mush on a plate or a slice of it on the side and you’ve got an Instagram-worthy dish and (apparently) a healthy meal. But before you stick another chip in guacamole, there’s something you should know: the planet is not meant to grow this many avocados.

A little research on the web and in a world of agricolture data can help unveil the huge impact avocado production has; what it is possible to find here is a perspective on the issue builted on three main topics: the growth of production, the international trade and the use of resources such as soil and water.

SUPERBOWL DAY

The Super Bowl effect usually impacts shipping volumes from the second and third week of January until it reaches its peak in the fourth week of the month. During this period through Super Bowl Sunday, West Park typically experiences a volume increase of up to 50%. According to the Hass Avocado Board, 204 million pounds of avocados were shipped into the U.S. during the first four weeks of January. Out of this amount of avocados, 93% were sourced from Mexico, 3.6% originates from California, 2.7% comes from the Dominican Republic while Chile supplied 0.7%.

AVOCADO ENVIRONMENTAL TROUBLES

In 2017 global avocado production is set to hit 5.8 million tons, up 3.4% from 2016, according to FAO reports. Increased worldwide demand is driving this growth for the fastest growing category of all tropical fruits. The average annual increase in growing area over the 2007-2016 period was 5.6%.

AVOCADO PRODUCERS

Avocado production is important to the economy of Mexico with the country being the world's largest producer of the crop. Mexico supplies 45% of the international avocado market. Of the 57 avocado producing countries, the other major producers are Dominican Republic, Peru, Colombia, and Indonesia.

AVOCADO TRADE

In 2016, the USA imported over 800,000 tons of avocado, being an unchallenged global leader of avocado import. The Netherlands come in second with around 150,000 tons imported, however they exports close to 120,000 tons to landlocked neighbors, keeping roughly 25,000 for domestic needs. The UK, despite being one of the early avocado adopters, is experiencing rapid growth of interest and demand, 30% in the last year alone.

The surge in demand has subsequently brought a growing supply, and an ever growing number of exporting countries. Mexico has a clear competitive advantage - it is one of the few countries where avocados can be produced all

year round, and it is geographically closer to the the main global importer - the USA. Peru exported roughly 110,000 tons of avocado, making it the second most prominent producer globally (the Netherlands resells avocado to neighbor countries). Peru's exports are known to be slow; delivery times to Europe often damage its reputation.

Its southern neighbor, Chile, follows in third. Spain is the only major European importer which manages to meet a significant part of its domestic demand with national avocados. Due to the seasonality of the fruit, Spain switches from exporting to importing (from Israel, Peru and recently Africa) twice per year.

THE EXTENSIVE USE OF RESOURCES

Avocado's needs for special conditions, in combination with the ever growing demand for the fruit, provoke concerns by a growing number of environmentalists. Similarly to coffee production, avocado is accountable for deforestation in the areas where it is grown. The avocado tree also demands large quantities of water which already has raised alerts in areas of Mexico where water is scarce.

THE WATER FOOTPRINT

If you’re one to not care much about the environment, perhaps consider the innumerable reasons why avocado went from being just a tasty fruit to what some call “green gold”. According to several news sources, avocado growers and pickers in Mexico reported being extremely unhappy with the wages they receive, leading them to hold a workers strike and slowing down the supply of avocados into the U.S.

Or again it’s possible to consider other social effects, like the link between illegal deforestation (in order to grow avocados) and illnesses in local population in Mexico, the war over water resources in Chilean provinces, or the thefts of avocadoes that happen all over the world, especially in New Zealand. The value of avocado trade is enourmous, and has of course attracted the attention of drugs cartels, such known as the Caballeros Templarios (Knights Templar). So when you buy a Mexican avocado, a greedy share of revenue may well accrue to criminals.
